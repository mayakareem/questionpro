# Focus Groups

## Plain English Definition

Guided group discussions with 6-10 people who share similar characteristics, moderated by a facilitator to explore attitudes, perceptions, and ideas about a product, service, or concept. Participants build on each other's responses, revealing group dynamics and consensus points.

## Best Used When

- You want to observe how people react to ideas in a social setting
- Group dynamics and interaction will reveal insights (e.g., how teams make decisions)
- You're testing creative concepts, messaging, or branding
- You need fast, directional feedback on multiple ideas
- You want to generate ideas through group brainstorming
- You're exploring cultural or social norms that emerge in conversation
- You need qualitative depth but have limited time vs 1-on-1 interviews

## Not Ideal When

- You need individual, uninfluenced opinions (groupthink can skew results)
- The topic is sensitive or personal (people won't open up in groups)
- You need statistically valid data or precise measurements
- You have a very small or hard-to-recruit audience
- Dominant personalities might silence others and bias results
- You need to understand individual customer journeys in detail
- Remote participants make facilitation difficult

## Typical Business Questions It Answers

- "How will customers react to this new brand positioning?"
- "What do people think about our competitor's latest product?"
- "Which of these three campaign concepts resonates most?"
- "How do buying committees actually discuss vendor selection?"
- "What associations do people have with our brand?"
- "Which features generate the most excitement in our new product demo?"
- "Why do customers prefer competitor A over us?"

## Sample / Audience Guidance

**Sample Size:**
- 3-5 focus groups (each with 6-10 participants)
- More groups if you have distinct segments
- Diminishing returns after 5 groups (themes repeat)

**Group Composition:**
- Homogeneous groups work best (similar roles, company sizes, industries)
- Mix of familiar and unfamiliar customers can create dynamic discussion
- Avoid mixing decision-makers with end-users in the same group

**Recruitment:**
- Offer incentives ($100-$300+ depending on seniority)
- Over-recruit by 20% (no-shows are common)
- Screen carefully to ensure participants fit target profile

## Core Design Elements

**Discussion Guide:**
1. Introduction (5-10 min): Set expectations, ice breaker
2. Warm-up (10 min): General topic discussion
3. Core topics (60 min): Dive into key areas with stimulus
4. Cool down (10 min): Final thoughts, ranking exercises
5. Total time: 90 minutes

**Facilitation Best Practices:**
- Professional moderator (skilled at managing group dynamics)
- Use stimulus materials (concepts, prototypes, ads)
- Encourage all participants to contribute
- Probe interesting comments ("Tell me more about that...")
- Watch for body language and emotional reactions
- Have observers take notes behind one-way glass or via video

**Logistics:**
- In-person or virtual (Zoom works but loses some energy)
- Record video for review
- Provide light refreshments (in-person)

## Expected Outputs

- **Video Recordings:** Full sessions with participant reactions
- **Transcripts:** Written record of all discussion
- **Top Themes:** 5-8 major insights with group consensus level
- **Concept Rankings:** Which ideas resonated most
- **Emotional Reactions:** How people felt about ideas (enthusiasm, confusion, skepticism)
- **Verbatim Quotes:** Powerful statements that capture sentiment
- **Highlight Reel:** Video clips of key moments for stakeholder presentations

## How To Interpret The Output

**Look For:**
- **Consensus:** When 70%+ of groups say the same thing, it's a strong signal
- **Passion:** Topics that generate animated discussion are important
- **Confusion:** If multiple groups don't understand something, it's a red flag
- **Unexpected angles:** Ideas that emerge from group interaction you didn't plan for
- **Segment differences:** How different groups react differently

**Important Caveats:**
- Loud voices can dominate—don't mistake volume for consensus
- Social desirability bias (people say what sounds good in public)
- What people say in a group ≠ what they'd do individually
- Can't quantify results ("3 out of 5 groups liked it" isn't statistically valid)

**Next Steps:**
- Test concepts that resonated in focus groups with quantitative research
- Refine messaging based on group feedback
- Address confusion points before launch
- Share video clips with exec teams (more impactful than written reports)

## Risks / Common Mistakes

**Risk: Groupthink**
- Dominant participants sway the entire group
- **Mitigation:** Skilled moderator redirects, uses round-robin techniques, anonymous voting

**Risk: Not representative**
- 30 people don't speak for millions
- **Mitigation:** Use focus groups for exploration, not validation; follow with surveys

**Risk: Researcher bias**
- Hearing what you want to hear in the discussion
- **Mitigation:** Have independent note-takers, watch full recordings, use text analytics

**Risk: Over-interpretation**
- "Everyone loved it!" when actually 2 people were enthusiastic
- **Mitigation:** Count how many mentioned each theme, note outliers vs consensus

**Risk: Logistics failures**
- No-shows, tech issues, poor facility
- **Mitigation:** Over-recruit 20%, test tech beforehand, have backup plans

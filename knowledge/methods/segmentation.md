# Market Segmentation

## Plain English Definition

A research method that divides your total market into distinct, meaningful groups of customers who share similar needs, behaviors, attitudes, or characteristics. Instead of treating all customers the same, segmentation helps you understand "who is who" so you can target, message, and serve each group more effectively.

## Best Used When

- You're launching a new product and need to know which customer groups to prioritize
- Your marketing feels generic because you're trying to speak to everyone
- You need to allocate resources (which segments get which products/services)
- Customer needs are diverse and a one-size-fits-all approach isn't working
- You want to personalize experiences but don't know how to group customers
- Executives ask "who is our target customer" and you can't answer clearly
- You're entering a new market and need to understand the landscape

## Not Ideal When (And What to Do Instead)

**Your market is homogeneous:**
- Problem: B2B with 20 similar clients, all Fortune 500 CFOs
- Use instead: Account-based research (interviews with each key account)
- Or: Skip segmentation, focus on understanding common needs

**You need quick tactical insights:**
- Problem: Segmentation takes 8-12 weeks, you need answers in 2 weeks
- Use instead: Quick survey on immediate question + simple demo splits
- Or: Use existing data (CRM, transaction data) for behavioral segments

**You lack resources to activate segments:**
- Problem: No budget for segment-specific campaigns or products
- Use instead: Prioritize understanding 1-2 key customer types (personas)
- Or: Wait until you can actually act on segment insights

**You already have behavioral data:**
- Problem: Your CRM shows clear purchase patterns
- Use instead: RFM analysis, cluster analysis on transaction data
- Or: Validate behavioral segments with attitudinal research

## Typical Business Questions It Answers

- "Who are our most valuable customer segments and how do they differ?"
- "Which segments should we target with our new premium product?"
- "How do we message differently to risk-averse vs growth-seeking customers?"
- "Which customer groups are most likely to churn and why?"
- "What media channels does each segment prefer?"
- "How large is each segment and what's their revenue potential?"

## Budget & Timeline Reality Check

### Standard Segmentation Study

**Cost:** $40-70K total
- Survey programming & fielding: $15-25K (800-1,200 respondents × $15-20 per complete)
- Analysis (factor analysis, clustering, profiling): $15-25K
- Workshop & activation planning: $5-10K
- Your time: 40-50 hours

**Timeline:** 8-12 weeks
- Week 1-2: Research design, questionnaire development
- Week 3-5: Survey fielding
- Week 6-8: Analysis (factor analysis, clustering, validation)
- Week 9-10: Profiling, sizing, naming
- Week 11-12: Workshop, activation planning

**Best for:** Strategic decisions affecting $5M+ revenue, launching new products, go-to-market strategy

### Segmentation Study + Ongoing Tracking

**Cost:** $60-100K (initial) + $30-50K annually
- Initial segmentation: $60-100K
- Annual re-fielding to track segment sizes: $30-50K/year

**Timeline:** 10-14 weeks initially, then quarterly updates

**Best for:** Large organizations with diverse customer bases, subscription businesses

### ROI Rule of Thumb

Segmentation ROI comes from:
- **Better targeting:** If you have 5 segments and focus on the top 2, you improve conversion 2-3x
- **Personalization:** Segment-specific messaging lifts response rates 20-40%
- **Resource allocation:** Stop wasting budget on low-value segments

Spend $50-70K if segmentation informs decisions affecting $10M+ in revenue or customer acquisition spend.

## Sample / Audience Guidance

**Sample Size:**
- Minimum: 800-1,000 (to support statistical clustering)
- Ideal: 1,200-1,500 (allows for segment validation and subgroup analysis)
- Larger if testing multiple markets or need rare segments

**Who to Target:**
- Current customers (understand who you already serve)
- Prospects (understand who you could serve)
- Lapsed customers (understand who you lost)
- Mix across demographics to ensure representation

**Recruitment:**
- Use QuestionPro panels for broad market reach
- Supplement with your customer email list
- Set quotas to ensure balance (don't oversample one group)

## Core Design Elements

**Segmentation Variables:**

1. **Attitudinal (needs-based):**
   - Values: convenience vs control, trust vs innovation
   - Priorities: price vs quality, speed vs service
   - Risk tolerance: conservative vs adventurous

2. **Behavioral:**
   - Usage frequency: heavy vs light users
   - Channel preference: digital vs branch
   - Purchase patterns: planners vs impulse buyers

3. **Demographic (descriptive):**
   - Age, income, location, life stage
   - Used to profile segments, not define them

**Key Questions to Include:**
- **Needs:** What matters most when choosing [product/service]?
- **Attitudes:** Agreement scales on category beliefs
- **Behaviors:** How often do you [use/buy]? Through which channels?
- **Demographics:** Age, income, location, industry (B2B)
- **Psychographics:** Risk tolerance, innovation adoption, lifestyle

**Analysis Approach:**
1. **Factor Analysis:** Group related attitude/need statements into themes
2. **Cluster Analysis:** Identify natural groupings of customers
3. **Profiling:** Describe each segment's demographics, behaviors, attitudes
4. **Validation:** Ensure segments are stable and meaningful
5. **Sizing:** Estimate size and value of each segment

## Typical QuestionPro Workflow

1. **Design Questionnaire**
   - Include 20-30 attitudinal/needs statements
   - Add behavioral questions (usage, channel, purchase triggers)
   - Capture demographics for profiling
   - Keep survey to 10-12 minutes

2. **Field Survey**
   - Use QuestionPro panels to reach 1,000-1,500 respondents
   - Set quotas to ensure diverse sample
   - Monitor data quality (remove speeders, straight-liners)

3. **Analyze Data**
   - Export data for statistical analysis (SPSS, R, Python)
   - Run factor analysis on attitudinal variables
   - Run cluster analysis (K-means or hierarchical)
   - Test 3-7 segment solutions to find optimal number

4. **Profile Segments**
   - Name segments based on defining characteristics
   - Describe demographics, behaviors, attitudes of each
   - Size segments (% of total market)
   - Estimate revenue potential per segment

5. **Validate & Activate**
   - Workshop with stakeholders to validate segments "make sense"
   - Create segment personas with photos, quotes, descriptions
   - Map segments to products, channels, messaging
   - Build targeting rules in CRM/marketing automation

6. **Track Over Time (Optional)**
   - Re-field survey quarterly to track segment sizes
   - Monitor if segment behaviors are shifting
   - Update targeting strategies

## Expected Outputs

- **Segment Definitions:** 3-7 distinct customer groups with clear names (e.g., "Value Seekers," "Premium Demanders," "Convenience Prioritizers")
- **Segment Sizes:** % of market and revenue potential for each segment
- **Segment Profiles:** Demographics, attitudes, behaviors, media habits
- **Needs & Motivations:** What each segment cares about most
- **Targeting Recommendations:** Which segments to prioritize
- **Messaging Guidance:** How to speak to each segment
- **Product Fit:** Which products/services appeal to each segment
- **Personas:** Visual 1-pagers representing each segment

## How To Interpret The Output

**Strong Segmentation Signals:**
- Segments are distinct (minimal overlap in attitudes/behaviors)
- Segments are sizable (each >10% of market, none >50%)
- Segments are actionable (you can target them differently)
- Segments make intuitive sense to stakeholders
- Clear "winners" emerge (high-value, accessible segments)

**Warning Signs:**
- Segments overlap heavily (not distinct)
- One segment is 80% of market (not useful for targeting)
- Segments defined only by demographics (not actionable)
- Stakeholders say "this doesn't match reality"
- No clear prioritization (all segments look similar)

**How to Use Results:**
1. **Prioritize segments:** Focus on 1-2 high-value, reachable segments
2. **Tailor messaging:** Create segment-specific campaigns
3. **Allocate resources:** Invest more in high-priority segments
4. **Product development:** Build features for target segments
5. **Sales targeting:** Give sales teams segment profiles to identify best prospects

## Risks / Common Mistakes

**Risk: Over-segmentation**
- Creating 12 segments when you can only realistically target 3
- **Mitigation:** Aim for 4-6 segments, prioritize top 2-3

**Risk: Demographic-only segments**
- Segments based only on age/income, not needs/attitudes
- **Mitigation:** Use needs-based or behavioral variables as primary segmentation basis

**Risk: Analysis paralysis**
- Spending months perfecting the segmentation, never activating it
- **Mitigation:** 80% solution that's activated > 100% solution on shelf

**Risk: Segments don't match existing data**
- CRM/transaction data can't identify which customers are in which segment
- **Mitigation:** Build a scoring model to classify customers based on available data

**Risk: No organizational buy-in**
- Marketing, sales, and product don't agree on segments
- **Mitigation:** Involve stakeholders early, run validation workshops

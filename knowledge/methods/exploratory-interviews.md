# Exploratory Interviews

## Plain English Definition

One-on-one conversations with customers, prospects, or stakeholders to deeply understand their needs, pain points, behaviors, and decision-making processes. Unlike surveys, these are open-ended discussions that let you discover what you don't know you should be asking about.

## Best Used When

- You're exploring a new market or customer segment and need to understand the landscape
- You want to uncover "why" behind behaviors, not just "what" happens
- You're developing new products or features and need rich, nuanced feedback
- You need to identify pain points, jobs-to-be-done, or unmet needs
- You want to understand the customer journey in their own words
- You're validating assumptions before investing in larger-scale research
- You need specific examples, stories, and context that surveys can't capture

## Not Ideal When

- You need statistically significant results or precise metrics
- You want to measure something across a large population
- You need to test specific feature variations or pricing
- You're on a tight deadline (interviews take time to recruit, conduct, and analyze)
- You already know what you're looking for and just need validation at scale
- Your budget is very limited (interviewing is time-intensive)

## Typical Business Questions It Answers

- "Why are customers choosing our competitor over us?"
- "What problems are we not solving that customers care about?"
- "How do enterprise buyers actually make purchasing decisions?"
- "What's the real onboarding experience like from a customer's perspective?"
- "What features would be most valuable to add to our product?"
- "Why did customers churn, in their own words?"
- "What jobs are customers hiring our product to do?"

## Sample / Audience Guidance

**Sample Size:**
- 8-12 interviews typically uncover most key themes
- 15-20 if you have multiple distinct segments
- Continue until you reach "saturation" (hearing the same themes repeatedly)

**Who to Interview:**
- Current customers (power users, churned users, new users)
- Prospects who didn't convert
- Decision-makers and end-users (both perspectives matter)
- Edge cases and outliers can reveal important insights

**Recruitment:**
- Offer incentives ($50-$200 depending on audience)
- Use customer lists, LinkedIn outreach, or professional panels
- Screen to ensure they fit your target profile

## Core Design Elements

**Interview Guide Structure:**
1. Warm-up: Build rapport, explain purpose
2. Context: Understand their role, company, goals
3. Experience: Walk through their journey, pain points
4. Specifics: Dive deep into key areas
5. Future: Needs, wishlist, hypotheticals
6. Wrap-up: Thank them, next steps

**Best Practices:**
- Semi-structured (have a guide but allow conversation to flow)
- 30-60 minutes per interview
- Ask open-ended questions ("Tell me about..." not "Do you like...")
- Use the "5 Whys" technique to dig deeper
- Record interviews (with permission) for accurate notes
- Have two people: one to interview, one to take notes

## Expected Outputs

- **Transcript Notes:** Detailed records of each conversation
- **Theme Summary:** 5-10 major themes with supporting quotes
- **Insight Report:** Findings organized by topic (pain points, needs, preferences)
- **Journey Maps:** Visual representation of customer experience
- **Persona Updates:** Refined understanding of who your customers are
- **Opportunity Areas:** Specific feature ideas or improvements backed by customer voice
- **Verbatim Quotes:** Powerful quotes for presentations and product briefs

## How To Interpret The Output

**Look for:**
- **Patterns:** What do 60%+ of interviewees mention? That's important.
- **Surprises:** Things you didn't expect are often the most valuable insights.
- **Intensity:** Not just what they say, but how strongly they feel about it.
- **Stories:** Specific examples are more actionable than generalities.
- **Contradictions:** When people say different things, segment by who they are.

**Red Flags:**
- One person's opinion is an anecdote, not a trend
- What people say they'll do ≠ what they'll actually do
- Leading questions can bias responses

**Action Items:**
- Group themes by "must-fix" vs "nice-to-have"
- Share compelling quotes with product and exec teams
- Use insights to inform survey questions for quantitative validation
- Identify hypotheses to test with larger-scale research

## Risks / Common Mistakes

**Risk: Small sample bias**
- 10 interviews can't represent millions of users
- **Mitigation:** Use this for discovery, then validate with surveys/data

**Risk: Interviewer bias**
- Leading questions skew responses ("Don't you think...?")
- **Mitigation:** Train interviewers, use open-ended questions, record sessions

**Risk: Analysis paralysis**
- Hours of interviews = overwhelming data
- **Mitigation:** Use text analytics tools, focus on top themes, timebox analysis

**Risk: Cherry-picking quotes**
- Finding one person who agrees with what you want to hear
- **Mitigation:** Report all major themes, note how many mentioned each

**Risk: Lack of action**
- Great insights that sit in a report and don't drive decisions
- **Mitigation:** Present to stakeholders with video clips, tie to metrics, create action plan

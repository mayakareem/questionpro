# Mystery Shopping

## Plain English Definition

A research method where trained evaluators pose as regular customers to objectively assess whether frontline teams deliver service standards, follow protocols, and provide the experience your brand promises. Think of it as a reality check on what actually happens when customers interact with your business.

## Best Used When

- You need to verify whether branches, stores, or service centers follow standards
- Training programs were rolled out and you want to measure compliance
- Customer satisfaction is dropping but you don't know why
- You're expanding and need to ensure consistency across locations
- Competitors are outperforming you on service and you need to understand gaps
- Franchisees or dealers may not be following brand guidelines
- You want objective measurement of frontline behavior (not self-reported)

## Not Ideal When

- You need to understand customer attitudes or perceptions (use surveys instead)
- The service is entirely digital with no human interaction (use usability testing)
- You're testing a new concept that doesn't exist yet (use concept testing)
- Sample size needs are very large (mystery shopping is expensive at scale)
- The business problem is strategic, not operational

## Typical Business Questions It Answers

- "Are our bank branches greeting customers and explaining digital services properly?"
- "Which retail locations are failing to execute promotions correctly?"
- "Are telecom stores following the sales process we trained them on?"
- "Why is NPS low at some hotel locations but high at others?"
- "Are our QSR franchisees maintaining food quality and cleanliness standards?"
- "Do our car dealerships offer proper test drives and financing explanations?"

## Budget & Timeline Reality Check

### Standard Mystery Shopping Program

**Cost:** $8-15K for 50 visits
- Shopper fees: 50 visits × $80-120 per visit = $4,000-6,000
- Platform/reporting: $2,000-4,000
- Your time: 20 hours (briefing, QC, analysis) @ $150/hr = $3,000

**Timeline:** 3-4 weeks
- Week 1: Scenario design, shopper briefing, scorecard finalization
- Week 2-3: Field visits
- Week 4: QC, analysis, reporting

**Best for:** Branch networks, retail chains, service center audits

### Ongoing Mystery Shopping (Quarterly)

**Cost:** $25-40K annually (4 waves)
- 200 visits per year × $100 = $20,000
- Platform + dashboarding: $5-10K
- Your time: 60 hours annually

**Timeline:** Continuous with quarterly reporting

**Best for:** Large networks (50+ locations), ongoing compliance tracking

### ROI Rule of Thumb

Mystery shopping ROI is measured in:
- **Service improvement:** If training costs $50K but mystery shopping proves only 30% compliance, you justify re-training
- **Revenue protection:** One bad branch experience can lose $100K+ in lifetime customer value
- **Operational fixes:** Finding that 40% of stores don't explain financing → fix process

Spend $10-15K if you have 20+ locations and service quality directly impacts revenue.

## Sample / Audience Guidance

**Sample Size:**
- Small network (10-20 locations): 1-2 shops per location = 10-40 visits
- Medium network (20-50 locations): 2-3 shops per location = 40-150 visits
- Large network (50+ locations): Sample representative subset (30-50%) with 2-3 shops each

**Who Should Shop:**
- Profile match to real customers (age, gender, affluence)
- Trained and certified mystery shoppers
- Different shoppers per location to avoid recognition
- Mix of scenarios (simple inquiry, complex purchase, complaint)

**Recruitment:**
- Professional mystery shopping companies (best quality control)
- QuestionPro field team with mystery shopping certification
- Freelance shoppers if budget-constrained (but requires rigorous QC)

## Core Design Elements

**Scenario Design:**
- **Objective:** What should the shopper do? (e.g., "Open a savings account")
- **Instructions:** Step-by-step guidance (arrive, wait, ask about rates, etc.)
- **Probes:** Specific questions to ask ("Can I link this to my existing account?")
- **Red flags:** Behaviors to watch for (failure to check ID, no product explanation)

**Scorecard Elements:**
1. **Environment:** Cleanliness, signage, wait time, ambiance
2. **Greeting:** Eye contact, friendliness, use of name
3. **Needs discovery:** Did staff ask qualifying questions?
4. **Product knowledge:** Correct information provided?
5. **Process compliance:** ID check, forms completed, disclosures given
6. **Closing:** Thank you, next steps, business card provided
7. **Overall impression:** Would you return? Recommend?

**Scoring Methods:**
- **Binary:** Yes/No (did they greet you?)
- **Likert scale:** 1-5 or 1-7 (how friendly was the greeting?)
- **Narrative:** Open-ended description of experience
- **Time stamps:** Wait time, transaction time

## Typical QuestionPro Workflow

1. **Design Scenario**
   - Define shopper journey (walk in, browse, ask questions, purchase/not purchase)
   - Create detailed checklist of behaviors to observe
   - Set scoring criteria for each touchpoint

2. **Build Scorecard in QuestionPro**
   - Use QuestionPro survey builder to create digital scorecard
   - Include observation questions, ratings, time stamps
   - Add photo capture for evidence (if permitted)
   - Set logic to flag critical failures

3. **Recruit & Train Shoppers**
   - Brief shoppers on scenario (video training recommended)
   - Conduct practice shops with QC feedback
   - Ensure shoppers understand scoring consistency

4. **Field Visits**
   - Shoppers complete visits and submit scorecards within 24 hours
   - Platform tracks completion and flags incomplete data
   - QC team reviews for consistency

5. **Analysis**
   - QuestionPro dashboards show location-level scores
   - Identify top performers and bottom performers
   - Analyze by touchpoint (greeting scores, product knowledge scores)
   - Flag systemic issues (e.g., 80% of locations fail to explain fees)

6. **Report & Action**
   - Share location scorecards with managers
   - Create training recommendations based on gaps
   - Re-shop low-scoring locations after training

## Expected Outputs

- **Location Scorecards:** Each location gets a score (0-100) with breakdown by category
- **Benchmark Comparison:** Top 25% vs bottom 25% locations
- **Touchpoint Analysis:** Which service elements are failing most
- **Verbatim Feedback:** Shopper observations and quotes
- **Critical Failures:** Locations that failed compliance (e.g., didn't check ID)
- **Training Gaps:** Areas where staff need retraining (product knowledge, greeting, closing)
- **Trend Data:** If ongoing, track improvement over time

## How To Interpret The Output

**Strong Performance Signals:**
- Overall score >80%
- Consistent scores across all touchpoints
- Positive narrative feedback ("staff was knowledgeable and friendly")
- No critical failures

**Warning Signs:**
- Overall score <60%
- High variance across locations (some excellent, some terrible)
- Critical failures (didn't check ID, gave wrong information)
- Low product knowledge scores despite recent training
- Long wait times or poor environment scores

**How to Use Results:**
- **Reward high performers:** Share best practices from top-scoring locations
- **Fix systemic issues:** If 70% fail on product knowledge, training is the issue
- **Coach low performers:** Provide specific feedback and re-shop after 30 days
- **Adjust processes:** If everyone fails to explain fees, the script may be unclear

## Risks / Common Mistakes

**Risk: Shoppers are recognized**
- Staff recognize mystery shoppers and perform differently
- **Mitigation:** Rotate shoppers, avoid repeat visits, use diverse profiles

**Risk: Scorecard is too long**
- Shoppers can't remember everything or complete 50-question surveys
- **Mitigation:** Limit to 15-20 key observations, focus on critical behaviors

**Risk: Scoring inconsistency**
- Different shoppers rate the same behavior differently
- **Mitigation:** Train shoppers, provide clear rating definitions, do calibration shops

**Risk: No action taken**
- Results sit in reports, nothing changes
- **Mitigation:** Share scorecards with location managers, tie to performance reviews, re-shop

**Risk: Ethical concerns**
- Staff feel "spied on" or mistrusted
- **Mitigation:** Be transparent that mystery shopping is used for training, not punishment

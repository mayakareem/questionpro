# Understanding Market Research

## 1. What market research is

Market research helps answer five core business questions:

1. What is happening?
2. Why is it happening?
3. Who is driving it?
4. What should we do next?
5. How do we track whether it worked?

Different methodologies solve different parts of that puzzle. Some are built to explore, some to measure, some to observe behavior, some to simulate trade-offs, and some to track change over time.

---

## 2. The major methodology families

### Qualitative research
Used to understand motivations, emotions, beliefs, barriers, decision journeys, and language.

Common methods:
- In-depth interviews (IDIs)
- Focus groups
- Ethnography / observation
- Online communities
- Diary studies

Best for:
- Discovering the “why”
- Exploring unmet needs
- Understanding behavior in context
- Generating hypotheses before quant

Typical outputs:
- Themes
- Verbatim quotes
- Journey maps
- Personas/archetypes
- Opportunity areas

### Quantitative research
Used to measure attitudes, awareness, usage, satisfaction, intent, and behaviors at scale.

Common methods:
- Online surveys (CAWI)
- CATI
- CAPI / face-to-face
- Intercepts
- Panels

Best for:
- Measuring “how many”
- Comparing segments
- Building trackers
- Testing concepts at scale

Typical outputs:
- Percentages
- Crosstabs
- Trendlines
- Dashboards
- Statistical analysis

### Observational research
Used to understand what people actually do, rather than what they say they do.

Common methods:
- Mystery shopping
- Ethnography
- Retail audits
- Journey observation
- Store/branch visits

Best for:
- Service delivery reality
- Retail execution
- Compliance
- Hidden behaviors and workarounds

Typical outputs:
- Scorecards
- Observed journey maps
- Operational gaps
- Training implications

### Experimental / trade-off research
Used when the business needs to optimize features, offers, or pricing.

Common methods:
- Conjoint
- MaxDiff
- Pricing research
- Monadic concept tests

Best for:
- Product design
- Offer optimization
- Prioritizing features
- Pricing strategy

Typical outputs:
- Ranked priorities
- Utility scores
- Willingness-to-pay
- Simulations

### Tracking research
Used to monitor brand, customer, or category performance over time.

Common methods:
- Brand tracking
- Satisfaction tracking
- Relationship studies
- Ongoing pulse surveys

Best for:
- Seeing movement over time
- Comparing against competitors
- Diagnosing performance change
- Supporting continuous decision-making

Typical outputs:
- Trend charts
- KPI dashboards
- Segment-level changes
- Executive summaries

## 3. Methodology cheat sheet

## In-depth interviews (IDIs)

**Objective:** Understand the deep reasons behind beliefs and behavior.  
**Best for:** Sensitive topics, B2B, healthcare, banking, premium purchases.  
**Collection:** One-on-one interviews by phone, video, or in person.  
**Ingestion:** Recording, transcription, coding, quote extraction, synthesis.  
**Output:** Themes, quotes, decision journeys, barriers, motivators.  
**Example:** Why affluent banking customers hesitate to move wealth.

## Focus groups

**Objective:** Explore shared perceptions, language, and group dynamics.  
**Best for:** Ads, concepts, brand perception, retail experiences.  
**Collection:** Moderated group discussions.  
**Ingestion:** Recording, transcripts, coding, comparison across groups.  
**Output:** Theme summaries, concept reactions, emotional responses.  
**Example:** Testing a telecom campaign before launch.

## Ethnography / observation

**Objective:** Understand real-world behavior.  
**Best for:** Retail, banking branches, home use, healthcare journeys.  
**Collection:** Shadowing, visits, diaries, photos, video.  
**Ingestion:** Field notes, media capture, behavior coding.  
**Output:** Journey maps, friction points, workarounds, unmet needs.  
**Example:** Understanding how customers actually choose at-home beauty services.

## Online communities / diaries

**Objective:** Track reactions and feedback over time.  
**Best for:** Innovation, co-creation, longitudinal feedback, concept refinement.  
**Collection:** Asynchronous tasks, journals, polls, videos.  
**Ingestion:** Platform tagging, coding, longitudinal synthesis.  
**Output:** Evolving themes, idea refinement, recurring pain points.  

## Online surveys (CAWI)

**Objective:** Measure attitudes and behaviors at scale.  
**Best for:** Awareness, usage, satisfaction, concept tests, segmentation.  
**Collection:** Online structured questionnaire.  
**Ingestion:** Programming, quotas, cleaning, weighting, analysis.  
**Output:** Tables, percentages, dashboards, significance tests.  
**Example:** Measuring awareness of a new real estate development among GCC investors.

## CATI

**Objective:** Gather structured survey data with interviewer support.  
**Best for:** Brand tracking, public-sector studies, B2B, healthcare, MEA markets.  
**Collection:** Interviewers call respondents and enter answers directly into a system.  
**Ingestion:** Sample loading, interviewer training, call monitoring, cleaning, weighting.  
**Output:** Toplines, crosstabs, trendlines, weighted results.  
**Example:** Quarterly banking brand tracker across UAE and KSA.

## CAPI / face-to-face

**Objective:** Collect structured data in person.  
**Best for:** Mall intercepts, low-digital populations, public services, events.  
**Collection:** Tablet-assisted in-person interviews.  
**Ingestion:** Field supervision, geo/time validation, uploads, cleaning.  
**Output:** Standard quant outputs with location-based cuts.

## Usage & Attitude (U&A)

**Objective:** Understand how the category works.  
**Best for:** Category entry, strategy reset, white-space identification.  
**Collection:** Usually a large quantitative survey, often preceded by qual.  
**Ingestion:** Category screening, usage, occasion, attitude, and brand-funnel analysis.  
**Output:** Category incidence, frequency, occasions, barriers, needs, brand funnel.  
**Example:** Mapping how consumers choose between salon and at-home beauty.

## Brand tracking

**Objective:** Track brand health over time.  
**Best for:** Banking, telecom, hospitality, retail, destination brands, government.  
**Collection:** Monthly, quarterly, or biannual surveys.  
**Ingestion:** Consistent questionnaire, trend database, weighting, dashboards.  
**Output:** Awareness, consideration, preference, imagery, trendlines, segment cuts.  
**Example:** Identifying why telecom consideration is falling despite awareness holding up.

## Segmentation

**Objective:** Divide the market into meaningful, actionable groups.  
**Best for:** Go-to-market, product design, media targeting, personalization.  
**Collection:** Large survey with attitudinal, behavioral, and needs-based variables.  
**Ingestion:** Factor analysis, clustering, segment profiling, validation.  
**Output:** Segment sizes, needs, behaviors, messaging implications.  
**Example:** Banking segments based on trust, convenience, and wealth ambition.

## Concept testing

**Objective:** Evaluate whether a new idea is clear, relevant, credible, and appealing.  
**Best for:** Products, services, ads, offers, bundles, app concepts.  
**Collection:** Survey and/or qual with mockups, descriptions, or videos.  
**Ingestion:** Stimulus exposure, diagnostics, open-end analysis, benchmarking.  
**Output:** Appeal, clarity, differentiation, intent, improvement areas.  
**Example:** Testing a premium global banking account proposition.

## Product testing / HUT / CLT

**Objective:** Test actual products or service experiences.  
**Best for:** FMCG, beauty, food, devices, healthcare tools, service pilots.  
**Collection:** Home use tests, central location tests, diaries, follow-up surveys.  
**Ingestion:** Logistics, compliance, rating capture, follow-up analysis.  
**Output:** Liking, repeat intent, strengths, weaknesses, comparison to competitor.

## Conjoint

**Objective:** Understand trade-offs between features and price.  
**Best for:** Telecom plans, banking packages, SaaS tiers, auto trims, subscriptions.  
**Collection:** Choice-based survey tasks.  
**Ingestion:** Attribute design, experimental design, utility estimation, simulations.  
**Output:** Attribute importance, utilities, winning bundles, willingness-to-pay.  
**Example:** Optimizing a telecom package.

## MaxDiff

**Objective:** Rank what matters most.  
**Best for:** Features, messages, service attributes, pain points, innovation ideas.  
**Collection:** Best-worst choices from subsets of items.  
**Ingestion:** Experimental design, scoring, segment cuts.  
**Output:** Ranked priorities with relative importance.  
**Example:** Determining which hotel attributes truly drive choice.

## Pricing research

**Objective:** Understand willingness to pay and acceptable price ranges.  
**Best for:** New products, services, subscriptions, premium upgrades.  
**Collection:** Structured pricing questions.  
**Ingestion:** Price curves, threshold modeling, elasticity analysis.  
**Output:** Acceptable range, optimal point, revenue implications.  

## Mystery shopping

**Objective:** Measure whether frontline teams deliver service standards.  
**Best for:** Bank branches, telecom stores, dealers, hotels, clinics, QSR, retail.  
**Collection:** Trained mystery shoppers complete scenarios and scorecards.  
**Ingestion:** Shopper training, validation, QC, scoring, benchmarking.  
**Output:** Location scores, KPI compliance, gaps, training recommendations.  
**Example:** Finding that bank staff greet customers well but fail to explain digital onboarding.

## Retail audit / shopper studies

**Objective:** Understand retail and shopper reality.  
**Best for:** FMCG, beauty, pharma, grocery, electronics, QSR.  
**Collection:** Store audits, shelf checks, exit interviews, receipt capture, images.  
**Ingestion:** SKU coding, photo validation, price normalization, metadata linkage.  
**Output:** Distribution, availability, share of shelf, promo compliance, shopper insights.  

## Continuous CX / satisfaction tracking

**Objective:** Monitor customer experience over time.  
**Best for:** Banks, airlines, hospitals, telcos, hospitality, public services.  
**Collection:** Transactional surveys, relationship surveys, open text, mystery shopping.  
**Ingestion:** Ongoing data feeds, text analytics, dashboarding.  
**Output:** NPS/CSAT/CES, root causes, touchpoint trends, action dashboards.

## 4. The data flow: collection, ingestion, output

### Collection
This is how raw information is captured:
- interviews
- group discussions
- surveys
- observations
- audio/video
- store visits
- phone calls
- product usage

### Ingestion
This is where a strong research services team proves its value:
- recruitment and screening
- sample management
- quotas
- interviewer or moderator training
- call monitoring
- transcription
- coding
- cleaning
- weighting
- modeling
- QA
- dashboarding

### Output
Output depends on method:
- qual = themes, quotes, journeys
- quant = tables, trends, significance
- conjoint = utilities and simulations
- MaxDiff = rankings
- mystery shopping = execution scorecards
- retail audit = shelf/store truth
- trackers = movement over time

---

## 5. How to choose the right methodology

Ask this first:

**Do we need to explore, measure, observe, simulate trade-offs, or track?**

- If the client does not know why something is happening → start with qual
- If they need to know how big something is → use quant
- If they need to optimize an offer → use conjoint
- If they need to know what matters most → use MaxDiff
- If they need to see whether stores or branches follow standards → use mystery shopping
- If they need to understand the market structure → use U&A
- If they need trends over time → use a tracker

## 7. The simplest way to remember it

- **Qual** = discover
- **Quant** = measure
- **Observation** = verify reality
- **Trade-off methods** = optimize decisions
- **Tracking** = monitor change over time

---

## 8. Final rule of thumb

A good research design should always answer:
1. What decision will this study inform?
2. Why is this methodology the right one?
3. How will the data be collected and validated?
4. What will the output look like?
5. What action can the client take immediately after reading it?

If a project cannot clearly answer those questions, it is probably not well designed.
